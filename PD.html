Major Dopamine Pathways

1. Nigrostriatal Pathway

Origin: Substantia Nigra pars compacta (SNc)

Target: Striatum (caudate and putamen)

Function: Involved in motor control and planning. Dopamine here facilitates smooth movement.

In Parkinson's: Primary site of degeneration, leading to motor symptoms.

2. Mesolimbic Pathway

Origin: Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA)

Target: Nucleus Accumbens

Function: Reward, motivation, and pleasure. Associated with addiction.

In Parkinson's: Less directly affected, but can influence mood and motivation issues.

3. Mesocortical Pathway

Origin: Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA)

Target: Prefrontal Cortex

Function: Cognition, executive function, attention, and working memory.

In Parkinson's: Can lead to cognitive impairments in advanced stages.

4. Tuberoinfundibular Pathway

Origin: Arcuate Nucleus of Hypothalamus

Target: Anterior Pituitary

Function: Inhibits prolactin release.

In Parkinson's: Dopamine agonists used in treatment can affect this pathway, sometimes causing side effects like hyperprolactinemia suppression.

Pathophysiology of Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the loss of dopaminergic neurons primarily in the substantia nigra. This leads to dopamine depletion in the striatum, disrupting the basal ganglia circuitry and causing motor symptoms such as tremor, bradykinesia, rigidity, and postural instability.

Key features include Lewy bodies (alpha-synuclein aggregates) and oxidative stress. Non-motor symptoms like depression, cognitive decline, and autonomic dysfunction may arise from involvement of other pathways.

Normal Brain

Healthy dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra produce adequate dopamine.

Smooth motor control via balanced basal ganglia activity.

No significant alpha-synuclein aggregation.

Parkinson's Brain

Degeneration of ~50-70% of SNc neurons before symptoms appear.

Reduced dopamine in striatum leads to overactivity of indirect pathway and underactivity of direct pathway.

Lewy bodies form, contributing to cell death.
